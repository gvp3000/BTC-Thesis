Why Market Crashes Create the Best Opportunities — Raoul Pal & Alex Gurevich
Raoul Pal interviews Alex Gurevich (CIO, HonTe Investments) about his updated book "The Next Perfect Trade." Deep discussion of crisis management discipline, the "concurrent necessity" framework for constructing trades that can't lose both sides, and why AI-driven deflation makes UBI and massive fiscal expansion politically inevitable. Gurevich argues the Fed can always print faster than AI deflates — meaning your debasement thesis isn't invalidated by deflation, it's accelerated by it.
▶ Click to expand full notes
Key Points
  • "Concurrent necessity" — the Perfect Trade framework. Gurevich's core concept: construct two paired trades where if one fails, the other must succeed. This evolved from classic risk parity into something more powerful — not just uncorrelated positions, but positions linked by causal necessity. His 2002 example: long equities + long treasuries. If the economy recovered, stocks win. If it didn't, rates go to zero and bonds win. His 2014 trade: long USD + long treasuries — if the Fed tightened, dollar wins; if they didn't, bonds win at zero rates. The key insight is finding the macro fork where both paths lead to profit on at least one leg.
  • Crisis management: get psychologically neutral first. Both Raoul and Gurevich stress that the first move in a crisis is clearing your mind, not your book. Raoul would close every position (winners and losers) and go walk in Hyde Park. Gurevich's approach: get to "psychological neutrality" — ask yourself "if this morning was the first morning of my career and I was just given cash, how would I deploy it today?" The point isn't necessarily getting flat; it's breaking your attachment to existing positions so you can see the new opportunity the crisis has created.
  • Soros principle: government intervention changes the fundamentals. Gurevich invokes the Soros framework — when governments intervene in markets (QE, stimulus, bailouts), they don't just affect prices, they change the underlying fundamentals of the economy. This is why "the crisis creates the opportunity" — the policy response to a crash often creates a bigger and more predictable trade than the crash itself. The 2020 COVID response is the clearest example: the crash was chaotic, but the $5T fiscal + monetary response was a one-way bet.
  • Post-2020 and post-2022 were "easiest money ever made." Gurevich describes the post-COVID period as having produced extraordinary trading opportunities precisely because the government response was so massive and predictable. Similarly, the 2022 crypto crash created obvious deployment zones for anyone willing to buy when the crowd was panicking. The pattern: maximum fear + guaranteed government response = maximum opportunity.
  • AI deflation makes UBI and fiscal expansion politically inevitable. Core thesis-alignment moment: Gurevich argues that if AI displaces labor at scale (which the exponential charts suggest is happening by 2026-2030), 70% of the population won't have marketable skills. In a democracy, that's politically unsustainable — the result is some form of universal basic income or fiscal transfer. "We already know the playbook — it was COVID. It's going to happen again." This isn't a prediction; it's a political inevitability in a democracy where the majority loses economic agency.
  • "No matter how fast AI drops costs, the Fed can print faster." Gurevich's critical insight: AI deflation in goods and services doesn't kill the debasement thesis. The Fed's ability to expand the money supply is unlimited and faster than any deflationary technology. The distinction is between CPI deflation (goods get cheaper) and asset inflation (money printing inflates financial assets). Both can happen simultaneously — and in that world, BTC benefits from the asset inflation side while the real economy experiences deflation.
  • CapEx boom as the macro bridge. Raoul notes Taiwan semiconductor exports to the US are 5 standard deviations above normal — a lead indicator of the massive AI CapEx cycle still coming. This CapEx spending is the "final demand" holding the economy together as AI starts displacing labor. Data centers create construction jobs, energy demand, and infrastructure spend that temporarily mask the displacement effect. But once the build-out is done, the displacement accelerates.
  • Energy paradox: oil declining despite surging AI demand. Gurevich flags the puzzle: data center energy demand is spiking but oil prices are falling. His explanation involves OPEC dynamics and the expectation that nuclear and alternative energy will capture AI's incremental demand. This creates a divergence trade — long nuclear/energy infrastructure, potentially short traditional oil if AI demand routes through new energy sources.
  • Exponential forecasting failure is permanent. Both agree that humans are constitutionally incapable of forecasting exponential growth. Even people who intellectually accept the AI trajectory "don't really in their bones accept where the charts lead." Gurevich: "No human being alive is capable of fully accepting where these charts lead. We all say we do, but we don't." This means markets will chronically underprice AI disruption — creating persistent mispricing for those who can hold the position.
